Glossary

This guide provides definitions and explanations for terms used in the service.

Beds

Beds refers to adult social care beds in care providers and includes the following categories:

  • general residential
  • general nursing
  • dementia residential
  • dementia nursing
  • mental health residential
  • mental health nursing
  • learning disability residential
  • learning disability nursing
  • transitional care
  • young physically disabled

Capacity (care provider services)

The ability of care providers to deliver care and support services. This includes:

  • available places – the number of people a provider can support at a given time
  • workforce availability – the staff and skills needed to deliver care
  • service capability – the ability to meet different levels of need, including specialist care

Capacity can be affected by factors such as staffing levels, funding, demand for services, and regulatory requirements.

Domiciliary care

Care services provided to people in their own homes.

This covers a range of different types of support, including help with daily living tasks, medical care, and specialist services tailored to individual needs.

Joint Stategic Needs Assessment (JSNA)

This is a process that local authorities and their partner organisations use to understand the health and care needs of people in their area. It helps inform decisions about planning, commissioning, and improving local services.

It is a statutory requirement under the Health and Social Care Act 2012 for local authorities and their partners to prepare a JSNA.

Market Position Statement

This is a document that local authorities produce to set out the current state of the adult social care market and how they expect it to develop. It helps care providers and other organisations understand local demand, commissioning plans and opportunities to shape services.

A Market Position Statement usually includes:

  • population trends and future care needs
  • existing care services and any gaps in provision
  • commissioning priorities and plans
  • opportunities for providers to develop services

Population needs

The underlying needs of a specific group of people or a community in relation to adult social care. This includes:

  • met needs – where appropriate care and support services are in place to meet a person's requirements
  • unmet needs – where required care and support are not available or accessible, leading to gaps in provision

Transitional care

Transitional care in adult social care is the process of moving a person from children's services to adult social care services.

Unpaid care

Unpaid care is the voluntary help or support given to someone with a long-term physical or mental health condition, illness, or problems related to old age. It does not include care provided as part of paid employment.

This support can include help with daily activities, medical needs or emotional support. It can be given to someone within or outside the carer’s household.
