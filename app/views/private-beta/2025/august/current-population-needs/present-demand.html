Drivers of population needs

Population needs for adult social care are influenced by a range of factors, including the population's age structure, disability profile and rate of dementia diagnosis.

Areas with a higher proportion of older adults, people with disabilities or rates of dementia diagnosis typically experience greater pressure on services.

Suffolk has a larger proportion of people aged 65 and over (18%) compared to the national average (7%). This suggests the need for care services adjusted by population size in this local authority may be higher than average.

What ‘deprived in 4 dimensions’ means
A household is ‘deprived in 4 dimensions’ if all the following apply:

  • no one in the household has at least level 2 education and no one aged 16 to 18 years is a full-time student
  • any household member is unemployed or economically inactive due to long-term sickness or disability, and is not a full-time student
  • any household member is disabled
  • the household’s accommodation is overcrowded, in a shared dwelling or has no central heating

What ‘dementia diagnosis rate’ means
The ‘dementia diagnosis rate’ is found by dividing the number of people with a formal diagnosis of dementia by the estimated number of people expected to have dementia.

The estimated number of people expected to have dementia is worked out by combining:

  • the characteristics of the local registered population
  • studies on dementia prevalence by age and sex

Current capacity – care homes: local authority-level insights

The number of adult social care beds per 100,000 adult population provides an indicator of current care capacity. A higher number suggests more sufficient capacity.

Care homes in Suffolk have 18.4 beds per 100,000 adult population, compared to the East of England regional average of 12.2 per 100,000.

Suffolk care homes are operating at 85% occupancy, which is higher than the regional average (77%). This suggests a more limited capacity to meet population needs than the regional average.

Current capacity – care homes: care provider-level insights

Examining individual care providers offers insight into how their capacity compares with other care providers at local authority, regional and national level.

Care Provider A is a small provider with 20 total beds, compared to the median (53 beds) in Suffolk.

Care Provider A has 90% occupancy for all adult social care beds compared to the median occupancy of all care homes in Suffolk (85%), suggesting limited availability for new admissions.

Indicator definitions and supporting information

Find detailed information about each indicator, including data definitions, data source, update schedule, and any limitations to be aware of before using the data.

Indicators for population needs

  • Population size
  • Population age
  • Households where the property is owned outright
  • Households deprived in 4 dimensions
  • One-person households where the person is aged 65 or over
  • People aged 5 or over who provide unpaid care
  • People in bad or very bad health
  • Disability prevalence
  • Learning disability prevalence
  • Dementia prevalence
  • Estimated dementia diagnosis rate

Indicators for care home capacity

  • Adult social care beds per 100,000 adult population
  • Percentage of adult social care beds occupied
  • Number of adult social care beds in care provider location
  • Percentage of adult social care beds occupied in care provider location

Find more information on your local care market

Market Position Statement for Suffolk (opens in new tab)

Most local authorities in England publish Market Position Statements (MPS) to help care providers understand the local care market.

You can usually find these MPS documents on local authority websites.

MPS documents include information on:

  • supply and demand in the local care market
  • forecasts of future supply and demand for adult social care services
  • how the local authority will support the local care market to meet demand
  • how the local authority will engage with care providers on challenges in the sector, such as funding pressures
